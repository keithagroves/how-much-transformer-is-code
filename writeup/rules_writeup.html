Field report · explainable-ai · July 2026

Rules All the Way Down

We spent this project testing one stubborn idea: a neural network is just rules — millions of tuned knobs that prove some configuration of ordinary code could do the same job. We tried to write that code. This is what we found: where rules win outright, where they hit a measurable ceiling, and how much of a real transformer we ultimately got running as human-readable code.

TL;DR. Hand-built rulebooks plateau hard on open text (~0.22 next-token accuracy) but jump 4.3× when the input is structured. Opening a real transformer (Qwen3-0.6B), we located its induction circuitry, proved it causally, and replaced it with explicit code at 96% fidelity for verbatim copying — then measured how much of those heads' natural-text function is reachable by rules in our lookup class. The answer surprised us twice: first measuring ~32% on our fiction corpus, then discovering under robustness testing that the fraction is domain-dependent — ~45–70% on WikiText — and noisy at small ablation gaps. Only the algorithmic regime gives a clean universal: ~100% of repeated-text function flows through rule-nameable positions. Generalizing, we replaced 36% of all attention heads and 21% of MLP layers with readable code, healed the network around the prosthetics by retraining 65k of 596M parameters, and landed at +0.674 ± 0.007 nats (three seeds) on natural text with the induction regime better than the intact model. Two controls calibrate the claim: zeroing the same parts and healing plateaus 3× worse (the prosthesis carries real function), while content-free positional templates tie on natural text at this replacement depth — the cheapest third of heads is positional machinery, and template content earns its keep specifically at the algorithmic circuits, where positional attention cannot copy at all.

Act IImitation by rulebook, and the wall

The project began small: could readable code replicate what a small language model does? First target: sentiment. We had ministral-3:3b label 72 sentences, embedded them with qwen3-embedding:0.6b, and hand-wrote a three-line rule over two discovered axes — a polarity direction and an evaluativeness gate. It hit 0.944 leave-one-out fidelity, the same ceiling as logistic regression. On a held-out, independently authored test set the polarity axis generalized (0.951) while the neutral gate collapsed — an honest, instructive failure that set the project's tone: report the number that hurts.

Then the real attempt: predict the next token, from rules alone. We distilled a 1M-word corpus out of ministral and compiled it into n-gram backoff rulebooks — order-7 down to order-2, longest match wins. The scaling law was clean and merciless:

Around that wall we ran eight careful negative experiments — semantic re-rankers, embedding-distance scoring, polynomial context maps, grammar generalization, cache models, word profiles. Every post-hoc addition to the frequency-ordered rulebook lost. The graveyard taught us laws we kept re-meeting later, inside the network itself:

Law 1. Frequency order is unbeatable post-hoc. If your signal isn't in the conditioning context, re-ranking candidates with it only adds noise.
Law 2. Memorization beats smooth maps. A rule seen 3 times outperforms a polynomial fitted on 640,000 examples.
Law 3. Conditioning only pays when the state is discrete, known, and semantically massive.

One genuine discovery came out of Act I's geometry: prediction and meaning live in different spaces. The qwen embedding space that separates sentiment beautifully collapses function words into a hub (mutual cosine 0.899); a distributional space built from PPMI + SVD predicts better. Transformers agree — that's why input and output embeddings differ.

Act IIStructure changes the game

The turn came from a simple reframe: maybe the generator isn't weak — the input is lawless. We rebuilt the corpus under three rigid schemas (weather reports, sports recaps, market summaries) and watched the same machinery transform:

Extracting the rules revealed an elegant anatomy: the corpus is a template automaton — 56% of transitions deterministic rails (confidence 0.94), 44% branch points (0.39). 140 templates own 52% of the text at 0.826 accuracy: a 25× compression of the rulebook at two points of cost. From there we distilled question-answering the same way: 77 Q&A pairs from ministral became lexical rules with an abstention gate (refuse unless a long-context rule fires). Result: 100% recall on trained facts, honest "I don't know" on everything else, zero neural network at runtime. It all ships as a self-contained page — The Legible Model — where you can watch every token get chosen by a named rule.

Act IIIOpening the weights

Act II proved rules can do the job when the world is regular. Act III asked the inverse question: is that what the network itself is doing? We moved from black-box imitation to white-box surgery on real weights — Qwen3-0.6B, 28 layers × 16 heads, run in fp32 on Apple silicon (GPT-2 first, as a warm-up; our head hunt reproduced the literature's induction heads exactly, which told us the instruments worked).

Finding the circuit

Feed the model 50 random tokens repeated twice and prediction on the second half is only possible by find-the-last-occurrence-and-copy. Qwen's loss on the repeated half collapses from 14.44 to 0.69 nats. (Second-half positions only; repeated-random numbers elsewhere in this report are full-sequence averages — unpredictable first half included — which is why the same regime reads ~7.5–8 in the finale table.) Scoring all 448 heads for the induction attention pattern found the circuit: L16.H14 spends 0.979 of its attention on exactly the copy-source position. Zero-ablating the top heads proved causality — and revealed the first anatomical surprise: Qwen distributes the mechanism. Killing 6 heads triples the repeated-half loss; a full kill takes ~60 of 448, and it is astonishingly surgical: the repeated half returns to the no-information level (12.94, vs 0.73 intact) while the unpredictable first half of the very same sequences is untouched (14.37 ablated vs 14.30 intact).†

† Why ~14.3 nats rather than the uniform-distribution baseline of ln 151,936 ≈ 11.9: the strings are uniformly random rare tokens, and a trained language model concentrates its probability mass on plausible text — random junk sits exactly where it puts the least. Worse-than-uniform loss is the expected signature, not an error.

The veto

On natural text the circuit turned out to be smarter than the copy rule. Measuring its effect on the copy-candidate token directly: when the copy is the right continuation, the heads boost it (+0.19 nats). When it's wrong, they suppress it (−0.41) — remove them and the model likes the bad copy more. Per-head attribution split the circuit into a copier module and an inhibitor module, with a sharp asymmetry: copying has backups (ablate 8 copiers, others compensate); nothing backs up the veto. And the inhibitors aren't a separate organ — they're induction heads whose output is conditioned on match quality. The network hand-rolled the same abstention gate we built for our QA responder.

The prosthesis ladder

Then the experiment the whole project pointed at: excise the organ, substitute code, measure recovery.

The ceiling row is the finding — with a caveat we only earned later. On this evaluation chunk we took the heads' true attention, masked it to positions any lookup rule could name, and got 32% — our code was already there, and the remaining ~two-thirds flowed through positions no induction rule would pick. (Robustness testing later showed this fraction is domain- and chunk-dependent — see below — but the decomposition itself, and what lives in the un-nameable part, holds up.) Inspecting those positions was the most fun this project has produced. A rare, readable tier of fuzzy induction:

q='his' → attends to what followed 'His' (case folding)
q=' Below' → attends to what followed 'Below' (tokenization variant)
q=' wasn' → attends to ''t' after ' hadn' (negated-auxiliary class)
q='Mrs. Hollow' → follower of 'Mrs. Holloway' (name prefix)

…but the bulk was something else. On natural text these "induction heads" park 47–94% of their attention on the BOS sink — their off state — and their remaining value flows through a diffuse cloud whose favorite targets are, literally, the story's cast: Liam, Elias, Mrs Holloway, Eleanor, the letter, the will, Detroit. Entity tracking. But query-selective entity tracking — which character matters right now — and neither a recency kernel (5%) nor a uniform entity average (13%, redundant) captures the selection. That selection is the smooth core: the same wall as Act I, met this time from above.

Act IVThe replacement frontier

If one circuit can be part-coded, how much of the whole network can? We built a template library of nameable attention patterns — BOS sink, self, fixed offsets, last-punctuation anchor, duplicate-token lookup, sentence-start/current-sentence/previous-sentence windows, and the gated fuzzy match rule — and least-squares fitted every one of the 448 heads as a mixture. Median fit: R² 0.82. Heads were selected for replacement by measured substitution cost in both regimes, and after surgery the network was healed: retrain only the RMSNorm gains — 65,536 of 596,049,920 parameters — with the code organs active. (Yes, exactly 216; we checked. 28 layers × (two 1024-dim layer norms + 128-dim q-norm + 128-dim k-norm) + the 1024-dim final norm = 65,536. A genuine coincidence.)

Two clean negative results sharpened the method. Longer healing overfits past ~60 epochs (the constraint is data, not time). And unfreezing the 58M o_proj parameters diverges from epoch one — norms-only healing isn't a compromise, it's the recipe: touch the small calibration knobs, never the big matrices.

The MLP front

MLPs got their own prosthesis: replace a layer's output with a token-keyed lookup table. The per-layer cost curve is the crispest anatomical fact we found:

The combined hybrid, and the two controls that calibrate it

Finale: 160 coded heads plus 6 lookup-table MLPs installed simultaneously, one healing pass. Then the two controls the claim needed. First: zero the same components and heal identically — if healing alone could do the work, our templates were decoration. Second, the sharper one: refit the same heads on content-free templates (BOS, self, fixed offsets only — nothing that reads tokens) and heal identically — if that ties, the content in our templates is decoration.

The prosthesis is load-bearing; the content earns its keep only where the algorithms live. Against zeros, the verdict is decisive: healing recovers distribution shift but cannot manufacture function — the zero control plateaus 3× worse and its induction regime never recovers. Against positional-only templates, the verdict is humbling and clarifying: at this replacement depth the content columns tie on natural text. The 160 cheapest heads are cheap precisely because they are positional machinery — sinks and offsets — which cost-based selection finds first. We completed the comparison at k=192 and the tie holds there too (full +0.84 vs positional +0.80 natural), with content's edge appearing only as a small, consistent advantage on the induction regime (~0.15 nats at both depths). Template content matters exactly where Act III proved it does: at the algorithmic circuits, where the coded match rule recovers 96% of verbatim copying that no position-only pattern can express — position cannot say where the match was — with zero fitted parameters.

How far does it travel?

Everything above is fitted and evaluated on our own distilled corpus, so we took the hybrid — templates, lookup tables, healed norms, all frozen exactly as fitted — to WikiText-103 validation. The honest answer: it degrades. The full hybrid costs +1.58 ± 0.10 nats out of domain versus +0.674 ± 0.007 at home. Decomposing the failure told two different stories. The head templates and their healing transfer: heads-only lands at +0.57, roughly double its at-home cost — degraded but functional. The MLP lookup tables are the real casualty, for a mechanical reason: only 61.5% of WikiText tokens were ever seen in the fit data, and unseen tokens fall back to a mean vector. Broadening the fit corpus to include WikiText train text raised coverage to 78.3% and cut the full-hybrid cost to +1.23 — a straight line from coverage to cost. The limitation is real and worth stating plainly: the code organs are only as general as the text they were fitted on. But it is a fit-data budget, not a conceptual ceiling.

Does it replicate? Yes, no, and instructively

Every Act III–IV number above comes from one checkpoint, so we ran the three headline measurements on a second family: Pythia-410M — GPT-NeoX architecture, different lab, different training data. The induction anatomy replicates (a near-pure head at 0.97; the mechanism distributed across 29 of 384 heads, though the cluster sits earlier in the network). The MLP result replicates in substance with a nuance worth keeping: the broad middle is cheap token-lookup everywhere (median +0.05) and the final layer is expensive in both models, but the expensive early "composition band" moves — layer 2 in Qwen, layers 5–6 in Pythia. On the same fiction chunk, the ceiling read 29%/75% for Pythia versus Qwen's 32%/67% — which briefly looked like a beautiful cross-family constant.

Then we stress-tested it, and the constant dissolved. A robustness suite — three models (adding Qwen3-1.7B, a 4× scale step), two text domains, eight chunks each — showed the "30/70 split" was substantially an artifact of measuring one shared fiction chunk. The nameable fraction is domain-dependent: on WikiText it rises to ~47% (Qwen-0.6B) and ~72% (Pythia) — encyclopedic text repeats entities and terminology verbatim, which is exactly what a lookup rule can name, while our entity-heavy fiction pushed function into diffuse entity-tracking. Per-chunk variance is large wherever the ablation gap is small (~0.1 nats), and at 1.7B the short-context gap nearly vanishes, making ratio estimates meaningless. What survives cleanly: the algorithmic regime (~98–100% of repeated-text function flows through rule-nameable positions, tight across all models), the rough additivity of the two components, and the qualitative anatomy. The quantitative lesson we paid for and pass on: never headline a ratio of two small numbers measured on one chunk.

Act VMoving the dial

Domain-dependence is a correlation — different texts, same models. Could we make it causal? The clean version is a twin study: train two identical tiny transformers, one on natural stories and one on a perfectly lawful world of slot-filled schemas, and measure each. We built the twins (17M parameters, matched 5M-token budgets, shared tokenizer) — and hit an honest wall: at this scale the models never crossed the induction phase change. No induction heads formed, more epochs only overfit, and you cannot measure the substitution ceiling of a circuit that does not exist. Reliably growing induction from scratch wants more data and compute than the experiment's budget allowed. We report the negative rather than bury it.

So we tested the same hypothesis on the axis we could control: hold the model fixed (real induction heads, Qwen and Pythia) and vary the regularity of the evaluation text across three domains — narrative fiction, encyclopedic WikiText, and the maximally lawful Act-II schemas. The prediction was that the rule-nameable fraction should climb with the text's verbatim-repeatable structure. It did, monotonically, on both model families:

This is the through-line closing on itself. Act II found, from outside the network, that structuring the input moved text generation into the regime where rules win. Act V finds, from inside the network, that the same structure moves the model's own circuitry into the regime where rules can name it. The boundary between "code" and "weights" is not fixed in the architecture — it is set by how much lawful, repeatable structure the data contains. That is one claim, measured from both sides.

Act VIWhat is the diffuse part made of?

The un-nameable majority — the attention that flows to no position a lookup rule would pick — looked, under inspection, like entity tracking. So we asked the sharper question: is it low-dimensional? For each induction head we stripped the rule-nameable columns from its true attention, kept the diffuse remainder over the recent entities, and asked how well a small learned readout of the residual stream reconstructs it. That is a real result about representation: the part rules cannot name is not high-dimensional noise, it is a low-rank, readable entity-selection signal — recency plus one learned direction. One honest caveat on the magnitude: the 0.61 above is inflated by the sink-adjacent positions where these heads park most of their mass. In the concise companion paper we redo this scoring with the attention sink zeroed and the distribution renormalized, and the same rank-1 direction then climbs only toward recency, not past it. So the durable claim is qualitative — a single low-dimensional direction exists and carries the diffuse "which entity" structure — not the specific reconstruction score.

We then tried to push it one step further — to show that plugging this direction back in as a parameterized template recovers the head's function downstream — and ran the cleanest version we could before reporting the result. In a renormalized stage-2 test we reconstruct each head's true attention, keep the rule columns plus the learned entity mass, renormalize over the retained context, and push it back through the value matrix, measuring what fraction of the head's ablation gap the substitute recovers. It is negative: over five held-out chunks (pooled ablation gap 0.382 nats) the rule-only substitution recovers −25% of the gap and rule-plus-rank-1-probe −30% — both below zero, and the learned probe no better than (in fact slightly worse than) rules alone. Substituting part of a head's attention scores worse than deleting the head outright, whatever the normalization: a head's attention does not decompose into "rule part + entity part" that can be reassembled through its value matrix. So the stronger claim — that rules plus one direction crack the functional ceiling — is unproven, defeated by a substitution that is methodologically ill-posed in this regime. We report the representational finding and stop at it. (The functional question is answered a different way in the companion paper: a query-edit probe that intervenes on the head's query rather than reassembling its attention finds the direction is causally used on both model families, but neither dominates nor names the selection — a real but partial effect, not the clean functional substitution we hoped for here.)

What we now believe

The two-regime law. Wherever behavior is algorithmic and discrete — verbatim copying, template rails, memorized facts, positional routing, mid-network token enrichment — explicit code substitutes at 90–100%, sometimes with zero fitted parameters, sometimes better than the weights. Wherever behavior is smooth and query-conditional — which entity matters now, continuous relevance over context — code hits a ceiling. Act I met this wall from below; Act III measured it from above; Act V showed the boundary moves with how much verbatim-repeatable structure the text contains (fiction < WikiText < lawful schemas, monotonic on two model families); Act VI showed the smooth side is at least low-dimensional — recency plus one learned direction reconstructs the diffuse entity-selection attention — even though we could not prove that direction carries the head's function downstream. The boundary is real, it moves with the data, and the far side of it is readable representation whose functional role resists clean substitution.
The abstention law. Every route into the network found the same structure: a lookup mechanism plus a match-strength confidence gate. Fitted boost schedules, fitted attention gains, and the inhibitor heads all independently rediscovered the curve we had hand-built for our QA responder. Rules-plus-calibrated-refusal seems to be what this computation is.
The redundancy law. Capabilities the network can re-derive are stored redundantly (copying: backups everywhere); discriminative capabilities are not (the veto: no backups). Damage at scale is interaction, not solo failure.

Is any of this significant?

Calibrated honestly, as always. The induction-head anatomy, copy suppression, fuzzy induction, attention sinks, and "MLPs as key-value memories" are all documented phenomena — Act III's first half is careful replication on a modern model, not discovery. That much of attention is replaceable by simple patterns echoes the PAPA line of work; our positional-only control confirms it from inside our own pipeline. Three things are, to our knowledge, ours: the ceiling method for symbolic substitution — including the robustness result that the nameable fraction is a property of the text's repeatable structure, not a universal constant of transformers; the substitutability map of MLP layers; and the double-controlled replacement result — a third of a transformer's attention plus a fifth of its MLPs running as readable code after a 65k-parameter heal, where the zero control proves the prosthesis carries function and the positional control localizes exactly which templates carry content: the algorithmic ones. Error bars are done (three seeds, ±0.007); the WikiText transfer test exposed a real, mechanical limitation (lookup-table coverage) reported above rather than hidden; and the robustness suite retired our own favorite number, the "30/70 constant," replacing it with the domain-dependence finding; and the entity probe (Act VI) delivered half of what we hoped — the diffuse part is a low-rank, readable direction, but we could not show it carries the head's function downstream, because partial-attention substitution turned out ill-posed. The rigor checklist we set ourselves is complete, negatives included. The cleaner probe we hoped might turn Act VI's representational finding into a functional one — editing the head's query rather than reassembling its attention, sidestepping the decomposition that broke here — has since been run in the companion paper: it shows the learned direction is causally used on both model families, but it neither dominates nor names the selection, so the functional ceiling stands. What remains before submitting anywhere is presentation work (positioning against PAPA and the pruning literature).

What it could become

Nothing here ships at +0.68 nats on a 0.6B model. But the capability points somewhere: audit tooling (an X-ray reporting which fraction of a model is nameable algorithm — the analysis is the product, no hybrid deployment needed); behavior pinning (behaviors that live in code can't drift under fine-tuning and can be edited as diffs — our coded induction already beats the original heads); and, long-range, hybrid runtimes where the algorithmic scaffold is shared, cached, and inspectable, and only the smooth residue needs weights.

Methods, briefly

Models: qwen3-embedding:0.6b (geometry), ministral-3:3b (data source and distillation target), gemma4:e4b (corpus authoring), GPT-2 small and Qwen3-0.6B (surgery), all local. Surgery: PyTorch forward hooks on o_proj/v_proj/mlp, fp32 on MPS; ablation = zeroing head slices of the o_proj input; substitution = writing code-computed attention through the model's own value matrices. Healing: Adam on RMSNorm gains only, 24 natural + 2 repeated-random chunks, best-checkpoint on held-out. Every evaluation reported here is on held-out text; every headline number has an intact and a zero-ablation reference. All code lives in the project repo, one experiment per file, each importable by the next.
