How Much of a Transformer Is Code?

Which computations in a small language model can be replaced by explicit surrogates, at what cost, under a stated rule vocabulary — and where that stops. Short answer: the attention routing of a third of heads becomes positional templates and a fifth of MLP layers become context-free tables, but at a substantial cost (perplexity roughly doubles), and the fraction is a dial, not a constant.

Abstract We ask how much transformer computation can be replaced by programs in a declared, restricted instruction set, at a stated description-length and loss cost. On Qwen3-0.6B and Pythia-410M we replace the attention routing of heads — the QK-derived pattern, retaining each head's value and output projections — with mixtures of named positional operators, and MLP layers with context-free token maps, then adapt only 65k normalization gains. At one operating point we report two distinct quantities — the routing of 36% of attention heads is template-replaceable, and 21% of MLP layers are context-free (memoizable) — different senses of "replaceable," priced together but not summed. The cost is substantial: against an identically adapted intact model it is +0.88 nats on held-out text, roughly doubling perplexity (+0.64 [95% CI +0.57, +0.71] against the raw intact model, a comparison that pockets a domain-adaptation discount) — a price that suits an audit instrument, not a compression technique. Zero and position-shuffle controls show that the replacements preserve real position-specific function. A preliminary check at three sizes (Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B/4B) under a weaker matched protocol finds the healed cost within a ±0.3-nat band across the 6.7× parameter range (fair +1.83, +1.38, +1.92 nats; three points with a non-monotone middle — consistent with flat, not proof of it) while the zero control's margin varies by an order of magnitude, non-monotonically (4.1×, 6.2×, 1.2×), collapsing where heads-per-layer doubles — a deletion control's separating power is a property of the model, not the method, and must be re-established per architecture. A nested instruction-set frontier localizes that function: fixed positional operators recover most of what deletion destroys, sentence and punctuation structure add a smaller gain, and duplicate and induction operators add none in the substitutable set. The replaceable fraction is a dial, not a model constant: it depends on the evaluation distribution, permitted instructions, component search, adaptation budget, and tolerated loss. On induction heads, the rule-nameable share rises from fiction to encyclopedic to rigidly structured text. The most seductive residue rule — attend to the first entity introduced, matching 83–92% of head selections — was the attention sink in costume (0 of 281 once controlled); beyond it the entity selection resists recency, syntax, coreference, co-occurrence, and a learned rank-1 rule, and a query edit shows a low-dimensional direction is used but does not reconstruct the choice. We therefore call the residue un-named under this DSL, not intrinsically smooth.

Setup

A trained transformer is a pile of tuned weights; the question is how much of what those weights do is expressible in a restricted replacement language. There is no formal syntax-level boundary between “code” and a matrix multiply — a matrix multiply is code, and a readable rule may compile to one. Our operational distinction is therefore a hypothesis-class restriction. We declare a small replacement DSL (domain-specific language), fit a program in that language, install it in the forward pass, and measure its held-out cost. A component is replaceable only relative to a DSL D, a program-size budget B, and a tolerated loss ε; change any of them and the answer can change.

The pipeline, in one line: declare the DSL, fit a program per component, install the programs in the forward pass, heal by retraining only the normalization gains, and measure held-out damage against two references — the intact model and a zero-ablation of the same components. The next section gives the complete recipe with its constants; nothing downstream uses any machinery beyond it.

By "code" we mean an operation you can name and write down — not necessarily a short one, and the two surrogate families sit at opposite ends of that scale. The attention surrogate is genuinely compact: a mixture over a fixed 25-instruction vocabulary, a median of 24 nonzero coefficients per head — legible and small, a few dozen numbers you could read aloud. The MLP surrogate is the opposite: a token-indexed table of mean output vectors, ~3,400 entries of 1024 dimensions (~3.5M numbers) per substituted layer. That is legible as an operation — "look up this token's average effect and emit it" — but it is not compact, and a skeptic is right that such a table can carry a great deal of learned data without being manually specifiable. So "code" collapses three separable axes, and it is worth pulling them apart: explicitness (can the operation be named?), compressibility (how few parameters describe it?), and substitutability (how much loss when it replaces the learned component?). The attention templates score on all three — named, tiny, and swappable. The MLP tables score on explicitness and substitutability but only moderate compressibility: they establish that the layer is context-free (memoization of a per-token map), not that it is program-like. Because these come apart, we avoid the single word "code" for the combined claim and instead report two named quantities — template-replaceable attention and context-free MLPs — measured, and caveated, separately.

How a replacement program is made

Every number in this paper is produced by one six-step recipe. We spell it out once, with the exact constants, so that "creating code" is a reproducible procedure rather than a metaphor. Later sections each vary exactly one dial of this recipe.

1 · Declare the instruction set. The attention DSL is 25 named instructions, each a deterministic function of the token IDs alone: START (attend to position 0), SELF, OFFSET(k) for k = 1…16, LAST_PUNCTUATION, SENTENCE_START, CURRENT_SENTENCE, PREVIOUS_SENTENCE, LINE_START, PREVIOUS_DUPLICATE (uniform over earlier occurrences of a similar token), and FOLLOWER_OF_LONGEST_MATCH (score earlier positions by the length of the fuzzy match ending just before them and attend to their successors — the induction rule). Given a sequence x, each instruction emits a causal attention column Ck(x); the combinators are MIX, ROW_NORMALIZE, and APPLY_TO_VALUES. No instruction reads the residual stream, an activation, or a weight. The MLP DSL is two instructions — TOKEN_LOOKUP and GLOBAL_DEFAULT — with LOW_RANK_MAP and FREQUENT_TOKEN_EXCEPTION added for the compressed variant.

2 · Fit each attention head by regression. One forward pass over a 900-token calibration sequence records every head's actual attention matrix Ah. Compile the 25 instruction columns on the same sequence and solve a ridge-regularized least squares from columns to Ah, clipping negative coefficients to zero (the first 50 positions are excluded from the fit to avoid the attention-sink warm-up). Execution on unseen text rebuilds the columns there and applies the frozen coefficients:

w_h = clip_nonnegative(least_squares(C(x_cal), A_h(x_cal)))   # fit once
Â_h(x) = row_normalize(sum_k w_h[k] * C_k(x))                  # execute anywhere
Ŷ_h(x) = Â_h(x) @ V_group(h)(x)

The vector wh — median 24 nonzero coefficients — is the program (the worked example below prints one). Fitting is a single convex solve: no gradient steps, no search over program structure.

3 · Fit each MLP layer by memoization. Run the model over 24 calibration chunks of 600 tokens and record, for each vocabulary item, the mean output vector the layer produced on that token — ~3,400 entries per substituted layer. At execution, TOKEN_LOOKUP emits the stored row; tokens never seen in calibration fall back to GLOBAL_DEFAULT, the layer's grand-mean output.

4 · Select which components to replace. Substitute each head alone and measure the damage on the calibration text and on repeated-random strings (which isolate the copying circuits); rank heads by the average of the two ranks and take the cheapest k (headline operating point: k = 160 of 448). MLP layers likewise: substitute one at a time, keep the 6 cheapest of 28. The repeated-random criterion means the selection partly optimizes for preserving copy circuits — this does not leak into the headline, because the induction heads it protects are exactly the ones ranked expensive and left outside the substituted set. This solo-cost heuristic is greedy, and we treat its output as a floor — the frontier section shows joint selection substitutes more for the same loss.

5 · Install. A hook replaces exactly one thing per component. For a head, the compiled Âh(x) multiplies the model's own value vectors and overwrites that head's slice of the output-projection input: the query/key computation is retired, the value and output projections remain. For an MLP layer, the block's output is overwritten by the table row. Everything else in the network runs unchanged.

6 · Heal, then measure. With the programs active, fine-tune only the 65,536 RMSNorm gains (of 596M parameters; Adam, lr 3·10−4, 10 epochs over the 24 calibration chunks plus two repeated-random sequences); every weight matrix stays frozen. Report held-out loss above the intact model, in nats, next to the controls: zero-ablation of the same set, the position-shuffled-code heal, and the intact-model heal that prices the domain-adaptation discount.

The dials, mapped: the nested-DSL frontier varies step 1 (which instructions are permitted); the damage-vs-count curve varies step 4's k; the greedy frontier replaces step 4's heuristic; the Ship-of-Theseus check varies the order of step 5; the controls vary what step 5 installs while holding step 6 fixed. A claim of the form "36% of heads substitute" is therefore always an output of this recipe at stated settings, never a property of the model alone.

How much substitutes, and the two controls

Three quantities carry the paper; every other number in it is a robustness check or a protocol variant of one of these.

  • Unhealed substitution cost — install the programs, change nothing else: +1.61 nats.
  • Fair healed cost — heal the 65k norm gains with the programs active and compare against an identically healed intact model: +0.88 nats. This is the headline. The raw-intact comparison is +0.64 [95% CI +0.57, +0.71], but it silently pockets a −0.24-nat domain-adaptation discount, so we treat it as the variant, not the claim.
  • Margin over matched deletion — the healed programs (+0.70) against the strongest same-budget deletion baseline, joint-informed pruning re-healed identically (+1.27): the code carries function no matched deletion recovers, by ≈1.8×. (Both sides share the raw-intact reference, so the adaptation discount cancels in this comparison.)

Replacing the routing of the cheapest 160 of 448 attention heads plus 6 of 28 MLP layers — 36% and 21%, selected cheapest-first at a tolerated loss, so read these as one operating point and a floor set by the heuristic, not an intrinsic fraction of the model (a later section shows a smarter joint search substitutes more for the same loss) — costs +1.61 nats on held-out natural text before healing; retraining only the 65k RMSNorm gains brings that to +0.67, so the heal repairs about 58% of the damage. That +1.61 decomposes as +0.77 from the heads, +0.48 from the MLPs, and +0.36 of head–MLP interaction on top. Healing changes which part dominates: healed separately, heads-only falls to +0.14 and MLPs-only to +0.28 (the heal repairs 82% of the head damage), yet the full hybrid still heals to only +0.70 — so the residual cost is almost entirely the interaction (+0.29), the coupling between coded heads and coded MLPs that the per-channel norm heal cannot fully reconcile, not the organs in isolation. Norm gains can rescale and reroute residual-stream contributions, so the fair question is whether the heal manufactures the function or merely reseats the code's output — which is what the controls decide. (The healed figure holds out-of-sample: pooling eight chunks that trained neither the lookup tables nor the norm heal — and were not used to early-stop it — the healed damage is +0.64 nats, 95% CI [+0.57, +0.71], essentially the single-chunk +0.67. The unhealed decomposition above is from the headline chunk; what generalizes is the healed number.) A direct control settles whether the heal manufactures function: re-run it against the same code with its outputs permuted across positions — right statistics, wrong per-position function. Under an identical fresh-heal protocol, real code heals to +0.70 nats while the position-shuffled code heals only to +2.21 (95% CI [+2.08, +2.33]), approximately returning to the zero-ablation level (+2.12). Because the norm gains are per-channel they cannot undo a position permutation, so the recovery is functional, not the heal papering over scrambled code. (Substitution order does not matter: healing gradually, carrying the norms forward, ties healing all at once — +0.702 vs +0.705.)

Read the headline against the base rate: intact held-out loss is 3.38 nats, so +0.64 is a real degradation — perplexity rises from ≈29 to ≈55, close to a doubling — not near-parity. The claim is about what is substitutable at all at a fixed tolerance, and the controls show the recovery is functional; the number is not a free lunch. We report the +0.6–0.7 nat band as the operating point because that is where the norm-heal has converged and the zero, positional, and shuffle controls separate cleanly; tighten the tolerance and the substitutable set shrinks, loosen it and the headline inflates. The regime where this price makes sense is audit — a calibrated statement of what a component computes and what naming it costs — not deployment compression; a reader who needs near-parity should read the fraction at their own tolerance off the published curve.

There is one more asymmetry to net out, and it moves the number. The healed hybrid fine-tuned 65k norm gains on the corpus; the intact reference did not. Running the same heal on the intact model with no code installed improves its held-out loss by −0.24 nats (95% CI [−0.27, −0.21]) — pure domain adaptation, a discount the healed-code number collects for free against the raw-intact reference. Measured instead against an identically-healed no-code baseline — the fair comparison — the code substitution costs about +0.88 nats, not +0.64. We report both, and quote +0.64 as the raw-intact figure with this offset stated. The controls are unaffected: the zero-ablation and shuffled-code conditions are healed on the same corpus and share the same discount, so their gaps from the code condition — the load-bearing comparisons — stand exactly as reported. "The cheapest 160 heads" invites a circularity worry — of course the easiest heads to replace are the template-like ones. The full cost curve both concedes and complicates it. Individually, the model's heads are massively redundant: 427 of 448 cost under 0.02 nats to replace on their own, and 121 are free or negative (removing them alone does nothing, or helps). The whole +1.61 damage is therefore not a sum of solo costs — the cheapest 160 sum to −0.39 nats solo — but an interaction effect that emerges only when many are swapped at once. So the selection criterion (solo cost) and the thing healing must repair (joint damage) are different quantities, and the genuinely expensive per-head function lives in a thin tail: the two costliest heads alone exceed 0.1 nats each, and they are induction heads, not positional ones.

Sweeping the budget makes the "one operating point, not a constant" claim concrete. Substituting the cheapest-first k heads and measuring the damage traces a sharply convex curve: the first ~40 heads are essentially free, and the cost only accelerates as the budget reaches the expensive tail. So a smaller fraction genuinely costs less — steeply so — and 36% is a chosen tolerance on a rising curve.

If solo cost and joint damage are different quantities, is cheapest-solo-first even the right way to pick the 160? We checked at the boundary: a head's individual solo cost correlates only +0.14 with what it actually costs in context given the other 159, and several un-coded heads have negative marginal cost — adding them lowers loss. So the heuristic is demonstrably not optimal. Building a same-budget set that swaps the worst-contributing coded heads for the best un-coded ones cuts the unhealed damage from +0.68 to +0.54 (~21%; heads only, MLPs held fixed to isolate the head-selection question — hence +0.68 here rather than the +1.61 heads-plus-MLPs figure above), and the curve is still falling where we stopped. The improvement is real but modest — far below the 0.45 nats a naive additive account predicts, because the same interactions that motivate the question also eat most of the gain. The honest reading is that our "36% of heads" undercounts what a smarter joint search could substitute for the same loss; the reported figure is a floor set by a simple heuristic, not a frontier. Measuring that gap directly: heal a 200-head superset, rank every head by its post-heal marginal harm (leave-one-out on the healed model — the criterion that matters, since healing rescues most head damage), then re-heal the best subset. The 160 least-harmful heads heal to +0.57 against the fixed cheapest-160's +0.70 — a 19% better frontier at the same budget — the best 120 reach +0.45, and the 47 heads that are individually non-harmful heal to just +0.33. The floor is real, and a post-heal-ranked selection clearly beats it; the substitutable frontier is wider than the cheapest-first heuristic finds. And the positional-tie result is not an artifact of that heuristic: re-running the content-free control on the joint-improved set leaves it a dead tie (full code +0.63 vs positional-only +0.63, healed), so the better-chosen heads are also mostly positional — the joint search pulls in redundant positional machinery, not the content-bearing induction circuits, which stay in the expensive tail it never reaches.

Three nearby heads-only figures use the same 160-head replacement but different evaluation samples: +0.77 is the single headline chunk used for the heads/MLPs interaction decomposition; +0.68 is the four-chunk set used for the joint-selection study; and +0.74 is the eight-chunk pooled budget sweep. Their spread is sampling/protocol variation, not three definitions of damage.

This deserves stating without hedging, and a budget sweep settles it. Dropping the two content columns (duplicate-token and match-rule) from the fitted surrogate and re-measuring across the whole range, the fitted and positional-only curves never separate: the gap is +0.003, +0.002, +0.005, +0.003, +0.007 nats at 40, 80, 120, 160, 200 heads — indistinguishable from zero everywhere. So it is not that content ties at the chosen 36%; content buys essentially nothing anywhere in the substitutable range. The honest headline for the attention half is that the routing of 36% of heads is replaceable by purely positional templates (the heads' value and output projections are retained throughout); the fitted content terms earn their keep only in the ceiling experiments on repeated text, where induction heads do real copying — and those heads sit almost entirely in the expensive, un-substituted tail. So the "96% of verbatim copying" figure below is a ceiling measurement (a match-rule column reconstructing induction on repeated sequences), not a component of the +0.64 substitution; we keep the two separate rather than let the copy-algorithm's success borrow credit for the positional headline.

Relation to program synthesis

The current “synthesizer” is deliberately modest: humans specify the grammar, the program structure is a linear mixture, regression fits its constants, held-out substitution loss scores it, and a heuristic selects components. It does not discover arbitrary control flow, compositions, or new primitives. In program-synthesis terms this is sparse system identification over a hand-authored operator library. A fuller formulation is to search jointly over component set S and program P:

min_{P,S}  held_out_loss(model with P replacing S)
           + λ * description_length(P)
           + μ * execution_cost(P)

This connects to algorithm discovery systems such as AlphaTensor, which searches sequences of allowed instructions and rewards exact correctness plus efficiency. The analogy is useful but bounded: matrix multiplication has a proof-level specification valid for every input, while our replacement has distributional fidelity on held-out text. We synthesize an approximation to a learned component, not a provably equivalent algorithm.

The MLP result is anatomically clean and replicates across both model families: the middle of the network is nearly-free token lookup (one Qwen layer has negative substitution cost), while composition concentrates in the first two and last two layers. "MLPs as key-value memories" is not just true on average — it is a map, and the map has a shape. It is worth naming what the token-keyed table actually measures: context-independence — how much of a layer's output is a function of the current token alone, ignoring the rest of the stream. Read that way, "21% of MLP layers substitute" says a fifth of the layers are largely context-free per-token enrichment, which is a cleaner and more falsifiable claim than "run as code." Its compressibility is a separate, measured axis. The six full tables contain 20.74M parameters and cost +0.508 nats unhealed. A rank-64 global map plus exact exceptions for the 500 most frequent tokens costs +0.513 — within 0.005 nats — using 4.77M parameters, a 4.35× reduction. A smaller rank-64 plus top-100 program uses 2.31M parameters (~9× smaller) at +0.527. The structure is thus a moderately compact low-rank map with a frequent-token exception table, not either a raw memory or a short algorithm.

The ceiling, and why it is not a constant

Isolating induction heads and masking their true attention to only rule-nameable positions recovers a fixed share of their function. On a single fiction chunk that share came out ~32% for Qwen and ~29% for Pythia — a tidy cross-family agreement we briefly believed. A robustness suite (three models, two domains, eight chunks each) dissolved it: the fraction is domain-dependent, and the Qwen number in particular was an artifact of one chunk. Measured across eight, Qwen's fiction recovery falls to −2% (95% CI [−12%, +8%]) — no recoverable fraction is detectable under this rule vocabulary, with an upper confidence bound around 8%: on that model's hardest case the nameable share is at most marginal. Pythia's fiction holds up near ~46%. So there was never a cross-family constant; there was a noisy point estimate that happened to agree. Holding the model fixed and varying only the evaluation text isolates the dependence on the input distribution — it rules out model identity as the explanation, though selected-domain comparison is not itself a controlled intervention:

On our repeated-sequence benchmark, nearly all measured induction function flows through rule-nameable positions: in this setting the algorithmic copying behavior is almost fully code-expressible. Away from it, the fraction is a matter of degree, set by the input.

What the residue is — and how much is a limit of our rule language

The attention our first vocabulary could not name flows, on natural text, to a diffuse cloud over the story's entities — which character or object is currently in focus. Two questions follow: is that cloud nameable by a richer rule, and is it low-dimensional?

On the first, we widened the vocabulary with an entity-anchored rule (a cheap parser tags named entities) and re-measured the ceiling, against a control that adds the same number of random columns. Entity positions carry genuinely more of the head's function than random ones — Qwen recovers 40% of the induction gap from entity columns versus 19% from random, Pythia 67% versus 59%. The entity-minus-random gap is a paired +21% (95% CI [+10%, +43%]) on Qwen and +8% ([+3%, +14%]) on Pythia — both clear of zero over a chunk bootstrap, though Pythia's margin is thin — so a real part of what a narrow positional/match vocabulary misses is nameable as "attend to the entity candidates." The lesson is that the ceiling is partly a ceiling on the rule language: widen the vocabulary and it moves. But it does not vanish, and the strict rule "attend to the most recent entity" adds almost nothing (+4–5%). What a rule can name is where the candidates are; which one the head selects is left over.

The sink in costume

Which candidate the head selects, though, has resisted every name we tried — including one that looked decisive and died by control. A learned rank-1 readout of the residual stream at first seemed to explain the selection (61% of the diffuse mass); a primacy rule — attend to the first entity introduced — matched the head's actual choice 83% of the time on Qwen and 92% on Pythia. Both were the attention sink in costume: the first entity sits next to the start token, where these heads park half to four-fifths of their mass as an off-state, and with the sink zeroed and attention renormalized over the real context, the primacy match falls to exactly 0 of 281 forced mid-passage cases while the re-scored learned direction never beats recency. Beyond those two, a full vocabulary of readable rules — recency, salience, re-mention, the clause's grammatical subject from a dependency parse, neural coreference, soft mixtures of all of them, and a query-conditional co-occurrence rule — fails to beat a weak recency bias (~0.09–0.14 top-1, two-to-five times chance but nowhere near a rule), scored by argmax or distributionally, on both models. What survives is causal but partial: editing the query along the learned direction shifts the head's real attention toward the entities the direction predicts, on both families (6/6 heads positive on each; exact sign and sign-flip permutation tests, p = 0.031; equal-norm random directions do nothing), so the direction is used — it just does not reconstruct the choice. The full chronology — each rule, its control, and its numbers — is in the appendix.

So the residue claim, at its final size: when the head attends into context at all, it lands on an entity most of the time — the candidate-set claim holds — but no rule in this vocabulary, under this soft-model class, at this scale, reconstructs which one. That is a bounded negative, un-named under this DSL, not evidence that the selection is intrinsically smooth. And the lesson the project keeps relearning is stamped on it: in small models an apparent symbolic rule must clear a positional and attention-sink baseline before it is believed — the one rule that looked like it named the residue was the sink.

Does the shape survive scale?

Everything above lives at ≤0.6B, and the natural worry is that the structure is a small-model artifact. As a first probe we ran the cheap headline checks on Qwen3-4B — the same family, roughly seven times larger. The shape holds, and nothing inverts. Induction is still there and still concentrated (a 13.7-nat repeated-sequence collapse; about 5% of heads carry it, the same fraction as 0.6B). The MLP substitutability map is intact — expensive first and last layers, a cheap deep middle — and if anything the redundancy grows: a quarter of the 4B MLP layers are free or beneficial to replace with a token lookup, against a handful at 0.6B. Per-head importance stays thin-tailed: 88% of the 4B heads are individually near-free to ablate, the function concentrated in a small tail. And the domain-dependent ceiling reappears — induction barely matters on fiction (its gap is near zero, as at 0.6B), while on WikiText and structured text 58% of it is rule-nameable.

Two things shift with scale, both worth noting. The ceiling's ordering compresses: WikiText catches up to structured text (both 58%) rather than trailing it as at 0.6B, and induction leans even more lightly on fiction — consistent with a bigger model handling narrative through more distributed, less copy-like machinery. These cheap diagnostics (induction concentration, the MLP map, the ablation tail, the domain ceiling) are a first look, run in bf16 with a cruder lookup surrogate on a single model.

The thesis-critical measurement — what a healed substitution actually costs at larger scale — has now been run at three sizes, as a deliberately identical protocol so the numbers are self-comparable: WikiText, positional-only templates selected by template-fit residual at a fixed budget (36% of heads, 21% of MLP layers), token-lookup MLPs, and a 12-epoch norm-only heal in bf16 on a free-tier GPU. This protocol is weaker than the paper's headline pipeline (its heal recovers far less, and nothing here compares to the fiction-corpus +0.88); what it licenses is the cross-scale comparison:

Two findings. First, the substitution cost itself is roughly flat across a 6.7× parameter range — fair cost +1.83, +1.38, +1.92, with the endpoints nearly equal and the middle model the cheapest of the three, and the control the right way around everywhere. This is preliminary — one family, a weaker heal, a different evaluation setup — but three sizes now say the same thing: positional-template substitutability is not a small-model artifact. Second — and this is the honest surprise — the control margin is wildly model-dependent and not monotone in scale: 4.1× at 0.6B, 6.2× at 1.7B (deleting 36% of heads is catastrophic on both, +7.3 and +8.2 nats after healing), then a collapse to 1.2× at 4B. Our first two-point reading — "redundancy grows with scale" — is falsified by the middle point. The cleaner hypothesis sits in the table's architecture column: the two models where deletion stays expensive share the same 28-layer × 16-head layout, and the collapse happens exactly where heads-per-layer doubles (4B: 36 × 32), giving each layer far more intra-layer redundancy for the norm heal to exploit. On this evidence the honest claim is not that scale weakens deletion controls but that a zero-ablation control's separating power is a property of the model, not of the method — it cannot be assumed, only measured. Our first guess at how to restore it at 4B — smaller deletion budgets — has been run and refuted: at a 20% budget (230 of 1152 heads) 4B code + heal costs +1.05 (fair +1.11) against zero + heal +1.18, margin 1.1×, unchanged. This was a short 6-epoch heal and both sides were still improving, but the direction is decided: the zero side was descending at least as fast as the code side, so a converged heal can only shrink this margin further — the refutation is conservative. Lowering the budget makes deletion easier to heal, not harder. If a margin exists at 4B it lives at high budget, where deletion must eventually overwhelm redundancy (at 0.6B it diverges past ~160 heads); the 50% run at 4B is the outstanding test. Two caveats bound both findings: the zero-heals had not converged at epoch 12 (still descending at 1.7B and 4B while the code-heals plateaued), so every margin is an upper bound on its converged value; and the pruning comparison inherits the 4B caveat — the ~1.8× margin over joint-informed pruning is measured at 0.6B and should not be presumed at architectures where deletion heals this well. So the scale evidence, stated at its true strength: the anatomy replicates, the healed cost holds roughly flat across three sizes of one family, and the zero control's power varies by an order of magnitude across those same sizes.

A budget sweep at 0.6B runs the same protocol at three head budgets (heal epochs vary with the runs — 6/12/20 — which matters little on the code side: the code-heal converges fast, and extending it from epoch 12 to 20 moves the result only ~0.03 nats):

Three readings. The margin holds at 4–5× at every budget on this architecture — including the low budget, where a recovering zero-heal has its best chance to catch up — so the 4B collapse is not a generic budget artifact of the method. (The contrast at matched budget is stark: at 20%, 0.6B's margin is 5.4× and 4B's is 1.1× — the budget axis does not rescue the 4B control.) Deletion damage grows roughly linearly with budget and becomes unhealable at the top (the 50% deletion heal cannot recover at all), while code degrades gracefully — reproducing, healed and on a different corpus, the nested-DSL frontier's unhealed finding that deletion diverges past ~160 heads. And the marginal cost of code is non-uniform in the opposite direction from the selection's ordering: the step from 20% to 36% costs +0.96 nats but 36% to 50% only +0.26 — the codability ranking front-loads the best-reconstructed heads, yet the later, worse-fit tranche is cheaper to replace, one more instance of solo rankings mispredicting in-context marginal cost.

What is new, and what is replication

Induction heads, copy-suppression, attention sinks, and MLPs-as-memories are established; that much of attention is replaceable by simple patterns echoes prior head-simplification work, which our positional-only control confirms from inside our own pipeline. The closest prior is PAPA (Hassid et al. 2022), which swaps pretrained attention for constant input-independent matrices post-hoc with modest loss — the same phenomenon as our positional finding, under a looser discipline: PAPA's replacement is an unconstrained matrix and its adaptation full fine-tuning, where ours is a named program, a 65k-gain heal priced against an identically healed intact model, and shuffle/positional/matched-deletion controls. Causal scrubbing (Chan et al. 2022) and circuit-faithfulness testing (Wang et al. 2023; Conmy et al. 2023) are stricter epistemics for whether an explanation carries function; substitute-and-heal deliberately trades that strictness for executable surrogates and explicit cost accounting — complements, not competitors. To our knowledge the new contributions are these: the substitution-ceiling method and its finding that the nameable fraction is a property of the text's structure rather than a constant of the model, and that the ceiling is partly a property of the rule language rather than the network; the cross-family MLP substitutability map, whose shape replicates at 4B; the double-controlled replacement result, where a zero control establishes that the code carries function and a positional control localizes which templates carry content — verified on both the cheapest-solo and the joint-search set; the observation that the reported fraction is a floor set by the selection heuristic, not a frontier (solo cost correlates only +0.14 with in-context marginal cost, and a better-chosen same-budget set substitutes for less loss); the preliminary three-scale substitution result — under a matched protocol the healed cost is roughly flat across a 6.7× parameter range (fair +1.83, +1.38, +1.92 nats) while the zero control's margin varies non-monotonically from 6.2× down to 1.2×, collapsing where heads-per-layer doubles — the second half being a finding independent of our thesis: a zero-ablation control's discriminating power is a property of the model under test and cannot be carried across architectures; and, most pointedly, the hardened residue claim — the "which entity" selection survives every nameable rule we tried, symbolic or soft, once the attention sink is controlled for, with its most seductive symbolic explanation (first-entity primacy) unmasked as the sink in costume. Taken together these are less a percentage than a method: a way to measure which computations admit a short executable description, at what cost, under a stated vocabulary — with the replaceable fraction falling out of those choices rather than the model alone.

Limitations and next steps

  • Mostly small models (≤0.6B), short context, single-digit chunk counts; ablation gaps on natural text are small (~0.1 nats), so downstream ratios are noisy. The scale checks above show the qualitative shape and a roughly flat healed substitution cost across 0.6B/1.7B/4B (under a weaker matched protocol), but the full ceiling and the query-edit were not repeated there, the 4B zero control loses most of its separating power at the 36% budget, and nothing here reaches the sizes people deploy. The headline corpus is synthetic fiction generated by a 3B model; the only natural-text runs (WikiText, weaker scale protocol) price the same budget higher (+1.83 at 0.6B) — part protocol, but we cannot rule out that real text is simply harder, and the quotable +0.88 is tied to the synthetic corpus. Larger models with long context would both firm up the replication and make the functional questions well-posed.
  • Healing gives the network room to reroute; the shuffle and zero controls bound what that room buys (it recovers function, not distribution) and the −0.24-nat domain-adaptation offset is why we report the fair +0.88. The caveat those controls leave open: healing produces a hybrid adapted around the surrogate, so this measures substitutability, not that the original network implemented the rule.
  • The clean causal test — train twin models on regular vs. natural data and compare their nameability — failed at our scale: from-scratch 17M-parameter models never crossed the induction phase change. That experiment is compute-gated, not disproven.
  • The causal query-edit is six heads per model, now on two families (Qwen gap +0.49, 95% CI [+0.35, +0.64]; Pythia +0.82, [+0.46, +1.16]). The cluster bootstrap at n=6 is anti-conservative, so significance rests instead on an exact test — 6/6 heads positive on both models, sign and sign-flip permutation p = 0.031. The intervals are wide and the steering is partial — it shows a low-dimensional direction is used, not a tight effect size or the full shape of the attention it steers.
  • The which-entity residue resists every rule we tried, but that is a bounded negative (argued above): it holds in this vocabulary, one soft-model class, at this scale, and rests on ~six induction heads per model. It could be higher-dimensional, nonlinear, or a circuit spanning components rather than featureless — "un-named," not proven smooth. Given the many exploratory rule families, a confirmatory untouched holdout would strengthen it; and the remaining attack is scale, not more rules.
  • Is code better than same-budget pruning? We tried three deletion sets, healed. Solo-cost pruning is worse than deleting the code set (+3.81 vs +2.12 — solo cost mispredicts joint damage, the +0.14 correlation); but a joint-informed set, ranked by post-heal marginal importance, heals to +1.27. So against the strongest pruning baseline, code + heal (+0.70) still wins — but by ≈1.8×, not the 3–5× a weak baseline suggests. Code carries function no same-budget deletion recovers, at a real but modest margin.
  • How compressible is the MLP replacement? A rate-distortion sweep gives it an effective description length. A hybrid program — rank-64 global map plus exact corrections for 500 frequent tokens — uses 4.77M parameters instead of 20.74M (4.35× smaller) while matching damage within 0.005 nats (+0.513 vs +0.508). At a more aggressive point, rank-64 plus 100 exceptions is ~9× smaller at +0.527. So the replacement has real low-rank-plus-exception structure, but it remains millions of numbers rather than a short program.
  • The most immediately useful direction needs no new science: an audit tool that reports, for any model, what fraction of its attention and MLPs is nameable algorithm — the analysis is the deliverable.
  • What a referee will press on, answered plainly. Confidence intervals resample chunks (eight held-out ~300-token chunks; cluster bootstrap) — not tokens, documents, or seeds. Each protocol uses one calibration sequence and one seed, so sensitivity to both is unmeasured; model- and calibration-level replication would be stronger evidence than chunk-level intervals. The 160-head operating point was chosen after seeing the damage-vs-count curve, where the controls separate cleanly — it is not pre-registered, and should be read as one point on the published curve. Programs are fitted and evaluated within a single domain per protocol; whether a program fitted on one domain substitutes on another, and whether any of this holds past the 300–900-token context regime, are untested. The healed substitution pipeline itself has run only on the Qwen family (0.6B and 4B); the Pythia replication covers the ceiling and the query-edit, not the substitution.

Appendix: the residue chronology

The main text states the residue result at its final size; this appendix preserves the order in which the hypotheses rose and fell, because the controls that killed each one are the evidence that the survivor means something.

The first tidy answer was low-dimensionality. Fitting a rank-r readout of the residual stream, recency alone captured 31% of the diffuse attention mass and a single learned direction seemed to nearly double it to 61%, suggesting a clean decomposition: a nameable "attend to the candidates" part plus a low-rank "which candidate" direction. But that number came from the positions where the head's attention is most concentrated — the sink-adjacent ones — and it does not survive the sink control below.

Reassembling part of a head's attention through its value matrix scores worse than deleting the head — attention does not decompose into reassemblable parts — so we intervene on the query instead. We reconstruct a head's true attention from the residual stream and its own weights (exact to one part in a million), edit the query at a single position along the learned selection direction, and recompute the real softmax over unchanged keys. Attention shifts toward the entities the direction predicts (correlation 0.41 with the readout's key-side score, against 0.73 for the readout's own prediction); an equal-norm random direction does nothing (−0.08); the effect scales linearly with edit size. The direction appears to be used by the head, not merely decodable from it: a cluster bootstrap over the six heads puts the true-minus-random gap at +0.49, 95% interval [+0.35, +0.64], clear of zero. It replicates cross-family: porting the same reconstruction to Pythia's GPT-NeoX circuit (fused QKV, partial rotary, no per-head QK-norm; reconstruction again exact to ~1e-6) gives a stronger effect on its six induction heads — correlation 0.63 against −0.19 for a random direction, gap +0.82, 95% interval [+0.46, +1.16]. A cluster bootstrap on six clusters is anti-conservative, so we lean on an exact test instead: on both models all six heads individually show a positive true-minus-random gap (6/6), which a two-sided sign test and an exact sign-flip permutation test both put at p = 0.031 — significant without trusting the interval width. Still, this is the paper's thinnest leg — six heads per model, and a 0.41–0.63 correlation against the readouts' own 0.73–0.82 leaves most of the steering unexplained — so we read it as a real but partial effect, not a full account.

A tempting rule then seemed to close the story. Asking which readable rule best predicts which entity a head selects — its top-attended candidate — one wins overwhelmingly over recency and frequency across both families: attend to the first entity introduced in the passage, matched 83% of the time on Qwen and 92% on Pythia. Widening the candidate window only sharpened it, ruling out a fixed positional lag. It looked as though even the "which one" selection had a name. It was too neat, and a control breaks it: the first entity in a passage sits near the start token, and these induction heads park half to four-fifths of their mass on the start as an off-state — the attention sink. Forcing the question — zero the sink, renormalize over the real context, require the first entity to appear genuinely mid-passage — the effect vanishes on both models: across 281 forced mid-passage cases (Qwen, six heads), the head's renormalized argmax lands on the first entity in exactly 0 of them (0/281, not a rounded small fraction). Once it is not the sink, it is never selected.

With the sink removed, we scored a whole vocabulary of readable rules against the head's actual choice — past hand-heuristics to a serious linguistic toolkit: recency, salience, "most recent re-mention," literal coreference, the current clause's grammatical subject (a spaCy dependency parse), and full coreference resolved by a neural model (fastcoref). None clears a weak recency bias. The best predictor is recency itself at roughly 0.09–0.14 top-1, and the two linguistically serious candidates land below it (clause-subject 0.04–0.05, neural coreference 0.06–0.07) on both models, at >90% coverage — so it is not that the rules rarely apply. Nor is it merely a top-1 effect: scored distributionally — how well a soft model reproduces the head's whole attention distribution over candidates — a soft weighting of all the nameable features does no better than recency alone. The query-conditional rule a skeptic reaches for next ("attend to the entity that co-occurred with the current query token," active on a third of cases) adds nothing over recency (mass-overlap 0.333 vs 0.331). And the learned rank-1 direction, re-scored under the sink control, climbs monotonically toward recency-only as it is regularized and never above it — the encouraging 61% was, like primacy, an artifact of the concentrated sink-adjacent positions. Within every rule family we could name, none, scored by argmax or distributionally, beats a weak recency bias.
