Interpretability · short paper

How Much of a Transformer Is Code?

Measuring component substitutability under a declared instruction set. Keith Groves · July 2026 · long version (field report) · code

Abstract Interpretability regularly claims that a network component "implements" an algorithm. We test such claims by substitution: declare a small instruction set, fit a readable program in it for each component, install the programs in the forward pass, retrain only 65k normalization gains, and price the damage. The goal is not efficiency — the replacements are worse in every practical respect — but audit: does a claim about what a component computes survive being executed in its place? On Qwen3-0.6B, the attention routing of 36% of heads (value and output projections retained) and 21% of MLP layers substitute at +0.88 nats against an identically adapted intact model — roughly doubling perplexity, on a synthetic narrative corpus; the same protocol on natural text (WikiText) prices +1.21. The substitutable routing is almost entirely positional: content-aware operators price at zero. A position-shuffle control shows the recovery is function, not healing. The most seductive rule for what resists — "attend to the first entity introduced," matching 83–92% of selections — was the attention sink in costume (0 of 281 once controlled). The replaceable fraction is a dial — of instruction set, selection, adaptation, distribution, and tolerance — and the contribution is the pricing apparatus, including where its own controls break.

1 · Introduction

In plain terms. We ask whether stories about what a transformer's parts do are true enough to run. We write tiny, readable programs that are supposed to do what individual attention heads and MLP layers do, splice them into the network in place of those components, and measure exactly how much worse the model gets. Nothing here makes a model faster or smaller — the substitutes are worse in every practical respect, and no reusable efficiency trick falls out. The point is that an explanation you cannot execute is a story, and one you can execute has a measurable price. This paper measures the price.

Problem. Mechanistic interpretability regularly claims that a network component "implements" an algorithm, but such claims are rarely priced: if the algorithm really carries the component's function, substituting the algorithm for the component should cost little. There is no formal boundary between "code" and a matrix multiply — a matmul is code, and a readable rule may compile to one — so the question must be operationalized. We define replaceability relative to a hypothesis-class restriction: a declared domain-specific language D, a program-size budget B, an adaptation budget, and a tolerated loss ε.

Why it matters. A calibrated answer bounds what interpretability can promise: which parts of a model admit short executable descriptions, what the descriptions cost, and where they stop. The same measurement doubles as an audit primitive — report, for any model, the fraction of components substitutable under a stated vocabulary at a stated tolerance.

Research question. Under a declared instruction set, which computations of a trained transformer can be replaced by explicit programs, at what held-out loss, and does the answer survive controls for healing artifacts, weak baselines, and scale?

Contributions.

  • A substitution method with fair-baseline accounting: fit programs in a declared DSL, install them, retrain only normalization gains, and compare against an identically adapted intact model.
  • A double-controlled replacement result: at one operating point the fair cost is +0.88 nats; position-shuffled programs heal back only to zero-ablation level, so the recovery is functional, and purely positional programs tie the fitted ones everywhere, so the substitutable routing is positional machinery.
  • A nested instruction-set frontier quantifying the marginal value of each DSL tier at fixed budgets.
  • A characterization of substitutable MLP layers as token-local (context-independent, memoizable), with a measured rate–distortion curve (low-rank map + frequent-token exceptions, 4.35× smaller at equal damage).
  • Evidence that the reported fraction is a floor set by the selection heuristic, not a frontier (post-heal joint ranking substitutes 19% more cheaply at the same budget).
  • A preliminary three-scale result: healed cost roughly flat from 0.6B to 4B under a matched protocol, while the zero control's separating advantage varies by more than six nats across architectures — deletion controls must be re-established per model.
  • A hardened negative: which entity an induction head selects survives every rule we tested once the attention sink is controlled, including a seductive primacy rule that the control unmasked as the sink itself.

2 · Related work

Induction heads and the circuits framework [1, 2], copy suppression [11], attention sinks [4], and MLPs-as-key-value-memories [3] establish the component vocabulary we build on; our contribution is to price such descriptions by substitution rather than assert them. Head-pruning work [5, 6] shows many heads are removable; we use its strongest joint-informed variant as a baseline and measure what programs preserve beyond deletion. RASP, Tracr, and Transformer Programs [7, 8, 9] relate transformers to programs in the opposite direction — compiling programs into weights or constraining training toward interpretability — whereas we ask how much of an ordinary trained model admits program replacement after the fact. AlphaTensor-style algorithm discovery [10] searches instruction sequences for provably exact, faster algorithms; we search a declared instruction set for approximate, legible replacements and measure the loss, a rate–distortion rather than correctness objective. Distillation [12] also replaces computation, but into an opaque student; our student is required to be readable, which is the point.

Two prior lines require direct confrontation. PAPA [13] replaces attention in pretrained transformers with constant, input-independent matrices post-hoc and finds performance largely survives — the closest anticipation of our positional finding (Synthesizer [17] learns input-independent attention from scratch, in the same spirit). The differences are the discipline, not the phenomenon: our replacement is a named, legible program rather than an unconstrained matrix; adaptation is a 65k-parameter norm heal priced against an identically healed intact model rather than fine-tuning; and shuffle, positional, and matched-deletion controls price what the replacement carries. PAPA shows attention is often input-independent; we show which named function each substitutable head computes and what naming it costs. Causal scrubbing [14] and circuit-faithfulness testing [15, 16] are stricter epistemics for whether an explanation carries a function — scrubbing resamples activations under the hypothesis's equivalence classes, whereas substitute-and-heal deliberately lets the model adapt around the surrogate (a concession we make explicitly: we measure substitutability, not mechanistic equivalence). We trade that strictness for executable surrogates and cost accounting against controls; the approaches are complements.

3 · Methodology

Approach. One six-step recipe produces every number (full constants in Appendix B). (1) Declare the DSL. Attention: 25 instructions, each a deterministic function of token IDs alone — START, SELF, OFFSET(k) k=1…16, LAST_PUNCTUATION, sentence/line structure, PREVIOUS_DUPLICATE, FOLLOWER_OF_LONGEST_MATCH (induction) — plus combinators. No instruction reads an activation or a weight. MLP: TOKEN_LOOKUP with GLOBAL_DEFAULT; compressed variant adds LOW_RANK_MAP and FREQUENT_TOKEN_EXCEPTION. (2) Fit heads by regression: one convex ridge solve per head from instruction columns to the head's observed attention, coefficients clipped nonnegative (median 24 nonzero terms; a real example: layer 5 head 15 = 0.44·SELF + 0.27·LINE_START + 0.13·START + …). (3) Fit MLPs by memoization: each token maps to the layer's mean observed output. (4) Select the cheapest components by solo substitution cost (a greedy floor, shown as such). (5) Install: the compiled attention multiplies the model's own value vectors and overwrites the head's slice before the unchanged output projection — QK is retired, V and output projections remain; the MLP output is overwritten by the table row. (6) Heal and measure: fine-tune only the 65,536 RMSNorm gains with programs active; report held-out loss above intact, beside the controls.

Data. Headline experiments: a synthetic narrative corpus generated locally by a 3B model (~1.4M characters); calibration, heal-training, and evaluation chunks are disjoint spans. Scale experiments: WikiText-103. Held-out evaluation is 8 chunks × ~300 tokens in all protocols; repeated-random token sequences probe copy circuits separately.

Implementation. Qwen3-0.6B (28 layers × 16 heads) in fp32 on Apple-silicon MPS with eager attention; Pythia-410M (GPT-NeoX: fused QKV, partial rotary) for cross-family checks of the ceiling and causal probes; Qwen3-1.7B/4B in bf16 on a free-tier Colab T4 with CPU-resident templates and gradient checkpointing. Substitution is implemented as forward hooks; no weight matrix is ever modified.

4 · Experimental setup

Baselines and controls. (a) Zero-ablation + heal of the same component set — does the program beat deleting it? (b) Position-shuffled code + heal — same output statistics, wrong per-position function; per-channel norm gains cannot undo a permutation, so recovery here would indict the heal. (c) Positional-only programs — drop the content-aware instructions. (d) Intact-heal offset — run the identical heal on the intact model to price domain adaptation. (e) Pruning — delete a same-size set chosen by solo cost, and by the stronger joint-informed post-heal marginal ranking, then re-heal identically.

Metrics. Mean held-out next-token NLL in nats, reported as damage above the intact model. The headline is the fair cost: healed substitution minus the intact-heal offset. Three canonical quantities: unhealed cost, fair healed cost, and advantage over matched deletion; all other numbers are robustness or protocol variants.

Hyperparameters. Calibration: one 900-token sequence (first 50 positions excluded from fits; ridge 10−4). Healing: Adam, lr 3·10−4, 10 epochs (12 in the scale protocol), over 24 chunks × 600 tokens plus two repeated-random sequences; 65,536 trainable parameters of 596M.

5 · Results

The routing of 160 of 448 heads (36%) plus 6 of 28 MLP layers (21%) substitutes at fair cost +0.88 nats. The unhealed cost decomposes as +0.77 heads, +0.48 MLPs, +0.36 interaction; healed separately, heads-only fall to +0.14 and MLPs-only to +0.28, while the hybrid heals to +0.70 — an arithmetic decomposition consistent with the residual cost being head–MLP interaction the 65k-parameter heal cannot reconcile (an inference from the decomposition, not a directly measured interaction term). Substitution order does not matter (gradual vs one-shot: +0.702 vs +0.705).

The headline survives natural text. Because the headline corpus is synthetic — which has one virtue: it is guaranteed absent from Qwen's pretraining, where WikiText is not — we re-ran the full protocol on WikiText-103. Real programs heal to +0.94 [+0.73, +1.22]; position-shuffled programs heal to +1.89 [+1.66, +2.16] — the spine control separates with non-overlapping intervals on natural text. With WikiText's own intact-heal offset (−0.27), the fair cost is +1.21 nats against fiction's +0.88: natural text prices the same substitution roughly +0.3 nats higher, not multiples (the scale protocol's +1.83 at this budget was mostly protocol, not corpus). The port also supplied two measured warnings. First, the solo MLP scan on WikiText selected early layers whose joint cost explodes (+3.49 nats for the solo-picked six versus +0.36 for the middle band, every solo cost ≈0) — our own solo-mispredicts-joint finding biting our own pipeline; we select the middle band, as the MLP map dictates. Second, refitting on different hardware with bit-identical tokens changed the fitted programs (attention R² median 0.75 vs 0.81, unhealed k=160 moving by +0.8 nats) — a measured instance of the fit variance our chunk-resampled intervals do not cover; the healed result largely absorbed it.

6 · Analysis

Ablations. The damage-vs-budget curve is convex: the first 40 heads are nearly free, and costs compound through interaction, not through individually expensive components. Selection is a floor, not a frontier: solo cost correlates only +0.14 with in-context marginal cost, and re-ranking a healed 200-head superset by leave-one-out marginal harm substitutes k=160 at +0.57 versus the cheapest-solo +0.70 — 19% better at the same budget. A budget sweep at 0.6B under the scale protocol (20/36/50% of heads) gives fair costs +0.87/+1.83/+2.09 against deletion at +4.5/+7.3/+10.0: the code advantage holds at +3.7 to +7.9 nats at every budget (the 4B collapse is not a generic budget artifact), deletion becomes unhealable at 50% (its validation loss worsens from epoch 1) while code degrades gracefully, and the marginal cost of code falls with budget (+0.96 nats for 20→36%, +0.26 for 36→50%) — solo fit quality mispredicts marginal cost here too. On 4B the advantage stays collapsed at 20% as well (code +1.05, fair +1.11, vs deletion +1.18 — a gap of only +0.13 nats; a 6-epoch heal, but the zero side was descending at least as fast as the code side, so convergence can only shrink that gap — the refutation is conservative): lowering the budget makes deletion easier to heal, not harder, so the remaining place a 4B advantage could live is high budget. The MLP tables compress: a rank-64 global map plus exact corrections for the 500 most frequent tokens matches the full 20.74M-parameter table within 0.005 nats using 4.77M parameters (4.35× smaller; ~9× smaller costs +0.02). The substitutable MLP structure is a moderately compact low-rank map with a frequent-token exception list — memoization, not a short program, and we label the layers token-local rather than "code."

Error analysis: the sink in costume. What resists substitution is concentrated and specific. Induction heads (~5% of heads) do real copying — a match-rule ceiling reconstructs 96% of verbatim copy on repeated text — but their natural-text function is dominated by selecting which entity to attend to, and that selection has defeated every name we tried. A primacy rule ("attend to the first entity introduced") matched the head's choice 83–92% of the time on two model families; controlling the attention sink — zeroing it and forcing the first entity mid-passage — drops it to exactly 0 of 281 cases. It was the sink in costume. With the sink controlled, recency, salience, re-mention, clause subject (dependency parse), neural coreference, soft mixtures, query-conditional co-occurrence, and a learned rank-1 direction all fail to beat a weak recency bias (0.09–0.14 top-1). A causal query-edit shows a learned direction is used (6/6 heads positive on both families) but explains a minority of the steering. The residue is un-named under this DSL — not shown to be intrinsically smooth.

Statistical uncertainty. Confidence intervals resample the eight held-out chunks (cluster bootstrap); they do not span seeds, calibration sequences, or models — so they cover evaluation noise only and understate total uncertainty, since fit variance is unmeasured. For the six-head causal probes, cluster bootstraps at n=6 are anti-conservative, so significance rests on exact tests: 6/6 positive on both families, sign and sign-flip permutation p = 0.031. One measured protocol perturbation (moving 2 chunks from train to validation and adding early stopping, same model) moved the code number by ±0.16 nats but the zero-ablation baseline by 3 nats: the substitution result is protocol-stable, deletion baselines are not. The 160-head operating point was chosen after seeing the damage curve, not pre-registered; it is one point on a published curve.

7 · Limitations

  • Small models (≤4B), short contexts (≤900 tokens), one seed and one calibration sequence per protocol; single-domain fits. The healed pipeline has run only on the Qwen family; Pythia replication covers the ceiling and query-edit. The headline corpus is synthetic fiction generated by a 3B model; the matched protocol on WikiText prices the same substitution at fair +1.21 versus +0.88 — a real but modest corpus premium of ~+0.3 nats, with the shuffle control intact. Synthetic text also has a virtue the natural-text number lacks: it is guaranteed unseen in pretraining, where WikiText is not.
  • Healing measures substitutability, not mechanistic equivalence: the healed hybrid is adapted around the surrogate, so the result does not show the original network implemented the rule.
  • The fair cost is substantial — perplexity roughly doubles. The claim is about what is substitutable at a stated tolerance, not near-parity.
  • The scale check is preliminary (one family, weaker heal, unconverged zero-heals), and it itself shows deletion controls lose power on some architectures. The small-budget direction has been tested and refuted (4B at 20%: gap only +0.13 nats); the decisive remaining follow-up is a high-budget (50%) deletion on 4B-class models, where deletion must eventually overwhelm redundancy.
  • The residue negative is bounded: this vocabulary, this soft-model class, six heads per model, this scale. A confirmatory untouched holdout would strengthen it.

8 · Conclusion

Under a declared 25-instruction DSL, the attention routing of a third of a small transformer's heads reduces to positional templates, a fifth of its MLP layers to token-local token maps, at a fair cost of +0.88 nats — real function, honestly priced, carried by programs a person can read. The fraction is not a property of the model alone: it moves with the instruction set (measured by a nested frontier), the selection procedure (floor vs frontier), the adaptation budget, the evaluation distribution (the induction ceiling runs from ~0% on fiction to ~78% on structured text), and the tolerated loss. We read the apparatus, not the fraction, as the contribution: a way to price interpretability claims — including where the apparatus itself breaks, since the zero-ablation control that much of the pruning literature leans on loses its separating power on redundant architectures and must be re-established per model. The measurement generalizes: run the same recipe with the same controls on any model, and read off what fraction of it is, under your vocabulary, code. What does not reduce — the entity selection at the heads that matter most — is where the next vocabulary has to grow.
